Spin To Win

An Ice Skating Story

Written by Dianna Yim.

Overview

Ice skating has been around for many years, known for its elegant and grace by the people who participate in the sport. There are many competitions for it every year, from the Rostelecom Cup to the Grand Prix finals. All of this information is collected by the International Skating Union and released to the public in PDF format. BuzzFeed took these scores and arranged them into either JSON and CSV format, as shown here.

The dataset provides information on how the skaters were scored, what was done during their performance, individual judge scores, as well as the nationality of the skater. Three files were used - performance, judged aspects, and scores for all aspects performed. The files provided included many columns that allowed me to transform them in a multitude of ways.

Most of the data analysis was done on the category in which the skater participated in, which are as follows: Men, Ladies, Ice Skating, and Pairs. In addition to this, there are some analysis questions in regards to the nationality of the skater, and the elements performed in their sequence.

Changes to Questions

Due to the fact that my questions were much too broad and not necessarily targeted towards my dataset, most of them were changed so that they could be answered in a more logical way with concrete results.

How many elements are attempted for each category (Men, Ladies, Ice Skating, Pairs)?

From the previous question, we drew the conclusion that men had the highest segment scores out of the four categories. I wanted to understand whether this had a link to the number of elements in their performances. First, I plotted the number of elements for each category:

It appeared that men did typically tend to skate more elements from the other categories. The maximum, minimum and average were then plotted to gather a better understanding of the distribution of elements.

With this and the previous section compared, a pattern begins emerging. Men seems to always have the highest, with Ladies and Pairs following closely behind, and Ice Skating always being the lowest. This is possible because Ice Skating cannot perform lifts, thus heavily restricting the elements they can actually perform, and placing more focus on the component aspect of the scoring.

Are countries consistent with high element and performance scores? Or do countries with high performance scores have low element scores?

From an anime that I watched (Yuri! On Ice), there was a heavy emphasis on skaters who could not perform well in elements would usually focus on components, as well as vice versa. I was wondering if this was actually true, or if the data would refute this. Below are the results.

I organized all of the data by country, and then graphed both the element and component scores side by side. Additionally, I added an extra bar that would show the difference between the two scores. Apparently, the hypothesis of countries only paying attention to either element or component scores was incorrect - from the graph, it is clear that the two scores tend to not vary more than a value of about 10.

Out of the winners, what elements in their performance do they have in common?

Since every performance has multiple elements embedded within it, and specific elements are worth more, there should be elements that are noticibly higher in terms of their appearance rate, as shown below. All of the elements are abbreviated within the dataset - however, this link provides all of the necessary information to decifer these codes and understand what aspect was performed.

For clarity reasons, all elements that appeared less than 20 times were removed from the graph. It is clear that every winner in every country performed a CCoSp4, or otherwise known as a Change Foot Combination Spin, except for France. This combination spin appeared almost 500 times within all of the competitions, displaying its importance to most ice skating sequences. France seemed to favour the ChSq1, which is the Choreo Sequence, the second highest element performed on the list. It appears that Germany did not do any of these within their winning sequences - however, when referring back to Figure 7, it is clear that they did not win many competitions as it is (only 21 in Pairs).

Which of the elements appeared over 70 times in all the competitions, and in what categories?

From the previous question we were able to ascertain which elements were popular in which countries. However, elements are relatively specific to the category in which they are performed in. The following chart displays this:

As noted in the previous question, the Change Foot Combination Spin is still the most common - however, it is only performed in the categories Ladies and Men. Upon further analysis, it is clear that specific elements such as STw4 (Set of Sequential or Synchronized Twizzles), RoLi4 (Rotational Lift), etc. are specifically for the Ice Dance category. There is only one overlapping element between all four categories, which is the Combination Spin as labeled on the chart. The only category which performs this element is Ice Dance, although it is actually available for all. This is probably due to the fact that the number of points it is worth is actually extremely low, and would not be worth doing in the other categories.

Did You Know...

- Ice Dancing and Pairs are actually very similar, except that Ice Dancing competitors cannot perform jumps and are scored heavily on components, whereas pairs are typically scored more based on their elements.

- Components (Interpretation, Composition) are scored between 0 and 10, but Elements (Jumps, Lifts, Combos) are scored between -3 and 3, under a scale known as Grade of Execution, or GOE.
