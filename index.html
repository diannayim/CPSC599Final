Spin To Win

An Figure Skating Story

Written by Dianna Yim.

Overview

Figure skating has been around for many years, known for its elegant and grace by the people who participate in the sport. There are many competitions for it every year, from the Rostelecom Cup to the Grand Prix finals. All the information from these competitions are collected by the International Skating Union and released to the public in PDF format. BuzzFeed took these scores and arranged them into either JSON and CSV format, as shown here.

The dataset provides information on how the skaters were scored, what was done during their performance, individual judge scores, as well as the nationality of the skater. Three files were used - performance, judged aspects, and scores for all aspects performed. The files provided included many columns that allowed me to transform them in a multitude of ways. The reason why I chose this dataset was to learn more about figure skating and understand how everything worked.

Most of the data analysis was done on the category in which the skater participated in, which are as follows: Men, Ladies, Ice Skating, and Pairs. In addition to this, there are some analysis questions in regards to the nationality of the skater, and the elements performed in their sequence.

Changes to Questions

My original questions are listed below.

  • Through the use of total segment scores, can we predict the all the categories within the program? (Junior vs Senior, Ice Dance vs Ladies vs Men vs Pairs, Free Dance vs Short Dance vs Free Skating vs Short Program)
  • Using only data taken for groups who performed in 2016 and 2017, which classifier and columns provides the most accurate results using a random seed of zero and a test size of 0.4?
  • When classifying and clustering only using element scores for performances, is it possible to distinguish between different competitions and programs?
  • Is there a correlation between countries and having high component or element scores, respectively?
  • Is there any significant correlation between the number of elements done in a performance, and the total segment score? What is the difference in the number of elements in a free skating program versus a short program?
  • Do men typically hold the highest score in terms of total performance points? Is there a difference in the number of elements attempted depending on the type of skating (Men, Ladies, Pairs, Skating), as well as for age (Senior, Junior)?

However, after receiving feedback from Professor Willett, I realized that my questions were much too broad, and also very misleading. I was attempting to predict information with arbitrary variables. For example, question 2 involved me being extremely specific with the random seed and test size without a true basis or explanation. Additionally, there were much less junior competitions than senior, making it difficult to predict specific information about that category. I then decided to move to questions where I could have more specific answers, and learn more about the dataset itself which was my goal to begin with. My questions are as follows.

  • Which category (Men, Ladies, Ice Skating, Pairs) has the highest score for segment scores?
  • How many elements are attempted for each category?
  • Are countries consistent with high element and performance scores? Or do countries with high performance scores have low element scores?
  • Which countries have won competitions, and how many have they won for each category?
  • Out of the winners, what elements in their performance do they have in common?
  • Which of the elements appeared over 70 times in all of the competitions, and in which categories?

Which category (Men, Ladies, Ice Skating, Pairs) has the highest score for segment scores?

Within this question, I wanted to discover whether specific categories tend to have higher scores, and which categories this was present in. I first plotted all of the segment scores with the skating type within each performance, and calculated the average for each category. This is shown in Figure 1.

The clear winner here is...

While the analysis provided me with a rough understanding that men typically scored higher and a shape of how the skaters were scored, it seemed that the Men category had more discrepancy within their category, and also more performances than Pairs. I then replotted this into a box and whisker plot that would allow me to gather more information, as shown in Figure 2.

Men have a much higher upper whisker than the other categories due to two specific skaters as labelled on the chart – Yuzuru Hanyu and Shoma Uno. Those two anomalies seem to skew the data slightly – however, it does not change that the average is 22.4 points higher than Pairs, the next highest. From this question, I began wondering if the number of elements skated for each category would effect this score, which leads into the next question.

How many elements are attempted for each category (Men, Ladies, Ice Skating, Pairs)?

From the previous question, we concluded that men had the highest segment score out of the four categories, and questioned whether the number of elements skated in a performance had anything to do with it. First, I plotted the number of performances in each category, as shown in Figure 3.

It is clear that Men typically actually have more performances than the other categories, slightly higher than Ladies and being a fair bit higher than both Pairs and Ice Skating. With this in mind, I plotted the number of elements skated in each performance against the category in which they belong in Figure 4.

From the above graph, it is clear that the minimum number of elements for Men, Ladies and Pairs is 7, with Ice Skating’s minimum being 5. However, when observing the Men’s section of this graph, there is a large amount of performances that actually have 13 elements, whereas the maximum for Ladies and Pairs are only 12. Additionally, in the 2016 Skate Canada International competition, an Israeli skater Daniel Samohin actually skated 14 elements. The interesting fact is that although he skated 14 elements, he only received an overall segment score of 151.91 and only placed 3rd in that overall competition.

Looking at the overall information the graph provides, it is clear that Men actually do tend to skate more elements than other categories, possibly contributing to their higher segment scores in question 1.

Are countries consistent with high element and performance scores? Or do countries with high performance scores have low element scores?

From an anime I watched (Yuri!!! On Ice), there was a heavy emphasis on skaters who could not perform well in element scores would typically focus on components, and vice versa. I grew curious about whether or not this was true in official competitions. In order to understand this better, I averaged both the element and component scores for all of the countries, then took the absolute value of the difference between element and component scores to create a third column. This allowed a bar chart to display the corresponding elements. The nation names were hidden for readability and since they are not necessary for the analysis. The result is shown in Figure 7.

As the graph shows, there is no large discrepancy between the element scores and component scores for any specific country. The largest difference is from Georgia, with their element score being 10.03 points higher than their segment score. However, the rest of the graph shows that the scores of the countries are relatively consistent, with no significant outliers.

Which countries have won competitions, and how many have they won for each category?

To kickstart things for this question, I first found the number of countries that existed within the dataset, as shown in Figure 8. The answer was 49.

Following this, I then filtered by rank for each program to show only the winner, and plotted the number of times that each nation, as shown in Figure 9.

Out of the all the competitions in 2016 and 2017, it is clear that Russia has won the most with 305 first places, followed by USA. In addition, it is clear that only 9 countries out of 49 have actually placed first with all the nations that competed.

In addition to finding the overall winner, I also wanted to see which country has won the most first places in different categories. Thus, I split the graph above by category as well and the result is shown in Figure 10.

From this graph, we can ascertain that Russia and Japan have both won many of the Ladies competitions. Russia also has also won the most Pair competitions out of all the countries. However, USA has the most wins in the categories Men and Ice Skating. Another interesting point to note is that only two countries have wins in all four categories – Russia and Canada. USA has zero wins in Pairs which is interesting.

With this information, I also wanted to find out what elements are popular between the winners, which leads nicely to the next question.

Out of the winners, what elements in their performance do they have in common?

For every performance, there are elements embedded within it, and specific elements should be staples. I assumed that there would be elements that would show up more due to their popularity, or possibly score boost that they could provide to the overall score. All of the elements are abbreviated within the dataset - however, this link provides all of the necessary information to decifer these codes and understand what aspect was performed. I filtered so only performances with rank 1 remained, then graphed the elements with color representing the nationality of the skater. However, there were some elements that made the graph extremely unreadable, so I further filtered this information so only elements that appeared more than 20 times shown. This is shown in Figure 11.

It is clear that every winner in every country performed a CCoSp4, or otherwise known as a Change Foot Combination Spin (Figure 12), except for France (which is explained later why). This combination spin appeared almost 500 times within all the winner’s performances, displaying its importance to most skating sequences. France did do the ChSq1, which is the Choreo Sequence, the second highest element performed on the list. It appears that Germany did not do any of these within their winning sequences - however, when referring back to Figure 10, it is clear that they did not win many competitions as it is (only 21 in Pairs).

While we now have information about elements skated by the winners, do these relate to all the skaters, even those that did not place? This leads into the next question.

Which of the elements appeared over 70 times in all the competitions, and in what categories?

From the previous question, we discovered that the Change Foot Combination Spin was the most common within the winners, and every winning country used this move with the exception of France. However, I wanted to see if there was a similar trend when comparing all of the elements within the competition. I chose the top 70 in order to ensure that the chart was still readable, and then colored by the category that the element was skated by. The result is shown in Figure 13.

As noted in the previous question, the Change Foot Combination Spin is still the most common within all of the competitions (executed 706 times in total) – however, now that the colors are representative of the categories, it is clear that only Men and Ladies use this within their competition. This explains why France did not have any of these elements within their winners, since their only wins were in Pairs and Ice Skating. In addition, some elements are actually only performed by specific categories such as STw4 (Set of Sequential or Synchronized Twizzles), RoLi4 (Rotational Lift), etc. These are solely used within the Ice Dance category. Out of all of the elements, the only one that can be performed in all four categories is the CoSp4 (labelled in Figure 13), or otherwise known as the Combination Spin. However, the only category that actually executes this move within competition is Ice Skating.

When comparing the top elements within Figure 13 and Figure 11, it is clear that the top 4 elements are shared, but after that, it begins to change in popularity.

Conclusion

The category that typically has the highest segment scores is Men, and this may be tied to the fact that within each performance they have more elements executed than the other categories. Countries tend to get scored relatively similarily for their element and component scores, as opposed what is presented in the anime I watched. Russia, by far, won the most competitions in 2016 and 2017, followed by the US. However, while Russia has the top number of wins in the Ladies and Pairs category, USA has the top wins in Men and Ice Skating. The only two countries that won in all categories is Russia and Canada. In Men and Ladies performances, there are a lot of Change Foot Combination Spins, and Ice Skating has a lot of unique elements that only appear in their category. The top four elements in the winners and overall competition are the same, but change after that.

Honestly, when I started to look at this dataset, I did not realize there were so many different elements incorporated into skating. However, I now have a newfound respect for anyone who understands this ridiculously complicated sport because there are way too many things to keep an eye on.

Did You Know...

  • Ice Skating and Pairs are actually very similar, except that Ice Skating competitors cannot perform jumps and are scored heavily on components, whereas pairs are typically scored more based on their elements.
  • Ice Skating came from the Waltz.
  • Components (Interpretation, Composition) are scored between 0 and 10, but Elements (Jumps, Lifts, Combos) are scored between -3 and 3, under a scale known as Grade of Execution, or GOE.
  • Skates used to be made of animal bones!
  • There are specific moves that Men have a much harder time executing than Ladies.
  • While Russia is a strong contender in skating, USA has won at least one medal in every event starting in 1948.
